# Sector Analytical Playbooks

This document provides sector-specific analytical frameworks for the major sectors represented in the LyraAlpha AI universe. Use these playbooks to deliver sector-aware analysis — a bank and a semiconductor company require fundamentally different analytical lenses even when their engine scores are identical.

---

## Technology & Semiconductors

### Key Metrics (beyond engine scores)
- **Revenue growth rate**: >20% = growth premium justified. <10% = re-rating risk.
- **Gross margin**: >60% = software-like (MSFT, GOOG). 40-60% = hardware/semi (NVDA, AMD). <40% = commodity hardware.
- **R&D intensity**: R&D/Revenue >15% = investing in future moats. <10% = harvesting mode.
- **Free cash flow margin**: FCF/Revenue >25% = capital-light compounder. Negative FCF in growth phase is acceptable only with clear path to profitability.

### Semiconductor Cycle
- Semis are deeply cyclical despite secular AI demand. The cycle: shortage → capex boom → oversupply → inventory correction → recovery.
- **Inventory days**: Rising inventory days = demand softening. 3 consecutive quarters of inventory build = cycle turning.
- **Book-to-bill ratio**: >1.0 = orders exceeding shipments (expansionary). <1.0 = orders declining (contractionary).
- **AI infrastructure spend**: Currently the dominant demand driver. Watch hyperscaler capex guidance (MSFT, GOOG, AMZN, META) — their capex plans directly determine NVDA/AMD/AVGO revenue.

### Valuation Context
- P/E 30-40 is normal for high-growth tech (>20% revenue growth). P/E 20-30 for mature tech. P/E >50 requires >30% growth to justify.
- **PEG ratio**: P/E divided by growth rate. PEG <1.5 = reasonable. PEG >2.5 = expensive even for growth.
- Forward P/E matters more than trailing for tech — the market prices future earnings, not past.

## Banking & Financials

### Key Metrics
- **NIM (Net Interest Margin)**: The spread between lending and borrowing rates. NIM >3% = healthy. NIM compression = margin pressure.
- **ROE**: >15% = well-run bank. 10-15% = average. <10% = underperforming or over-capitalized.
- **P/B (Price to Book)**: The primary valuation metric for banks. P/B >2x = premium (justified by high ROE). P/B <1x = market pricing in asset quality concerns.
- **CET1 ratio**: Capital adequacy. >12% = well-capitalized. <10% = regulatory concern.
- **NPL/NPA ratio**: Non-performing loans. Declining = positive re-rating catalyst. Rising = de-rating trigger.

### Rate Sensitivity Framework
- **Rate hike cycle (early)**: Banks benefit — asset repricing faster than liability repricing. NIM expands.
- **Rate hike cycle (late)**: Credit quality deteriorates as borrowers struggle. Provision costs rise, offsetting NIM gains.
- **Rate cut cycle (early)**: Banks hurt initially (NIM compression) but benefit from improved credit quality and loan demand.
- **Rate cut cycle (late)**: Loan growth accelerates, credit quality improves, provisions decline. Best phase for bank earnings.

### Bank vs NBFC vs Fintech
- **Banks**: Deposit-funded, regulated, stable but slower growth. Valued on P/B and ROE.
- **NBFCs**: Market-funded, higher growth but higher risk. Liquidity crises (IL&FS 2018, DHFL 2019) are the tail risk. Valued on P/B but with higher risk premium.
- **Fintech**: Revenue growth and user metrics matter more than profitability in early stage. Watch for path to unit economics.

## Energy & Oil/Gas

### Key Metrics
- **Upstream (exploration/production)**: Revenue driven by commodity prices. Breakeven cost per barrel is the key metric. If oil is at $80 and breakeven is $40, the margin of safety is high.
- **Downstream (refining)**: Gross Refining Margin (GRM) is the key metric. GRM >$10/barrel = strong. GRM <$5 = margin pressure.
- **Integrated (both)**: More stable earnings due to natural hedge. When oil prices rise, upstream benefits; when they fall, downstream benefits from cheaper input.

### Oil Price Framework
- **$60-80/barrel**: Goldilocks for most energy companies. Enough margin for upstream, manageable input cost for downstream.
- **>$90/barrel**: Upstream windfall but demand destruction risk. Also triggers inflation concerns → rate hikes → broader market headwind.
- **<$50/barrel**: Upstream stress, potential capex cuts, dividend risk. But downstream and consumers benefit.

### Energy Transition
- Traditional energy companies investing in renewables are in "transition" — valued on legacy cash flows + optionality on green investments.
- Pure renewable plays are valued on growth metrics (capacity additions, PPA pipeline) not current earnings.
- The transition creates stranded asset risk for pure fossil fuel companies with no diversification plan.

## Healthcare & Pharmaceuticals

### Key Metrics
- **Pipeline value**: For innovative pharma, the pipeline (drugs in development) can be worth more than current revenue. Phase 3 drugs have ~60% approval probability. Phase 1 = ~10%.
- **Patent cliff**: When key drug patents expire, generic competition can erode 80-90% of that drug's revenue within 2 years. Always check patent expiry dates for top revenue drugs.
- **R&D productivity**: R&D spend / new drug approvals. Declining productivity = spending more to get less.

### Pharma Sub-Sectors
- **Big Pharma (JNJ, PFE, MRK, LLY)**: Valued on pipeline + dividend yield. P/E 15-25 is normal. >25 requires blockbuster pipeline.
- **Biotech (growth)**: Often pre-revenue. Valued on pipeline probability-adjusted NPV. Binary outcomes around FDA decisions.
- **Generic/CDMO (Indian pharma)**: Valued on margin expansion + US market share gains. FDA inspection outcomes are binary catalysts.
- **Medical devices**: Recurring revenue models (consumables). Higher margins than pharma. Valued like tech companies (P/E 25-35).

### FDA as Binary Catalyst
- **Approval**: Typically 10-30% upside for the specific drug's revenue contribution.
- **Complete Response Letter (CRL)**: 15-30% downside. The company must address FDA concerns and resubmit.
- **Warning letter (manufacturing)**: 10-20% downside for Indian generics. Can take 6-18 months to resolve.

## Consumer (Staples & Discretionary)

### Staples (Defensive)
- **Volume growth**: The key metric. Price-driven growth without volume = consumers trading down. Volume growth >5% = healthy demand.
- **Gross margin**: Input costs (palm oil, crude derivatives, agricultural commodities) directly impact margins. Watch commodity prices as leading indicators.
- **Market share**: In staples, market share gains/losses are slow but meaningful. 1% share gain in a large category = significant revenue.
- **Valuation**: P/E 40-60 is normal for Indian staples (HINDUNILVR, NESTLEIND) due to consistency premium. P/E 20-30 for US staples.

### Discretionary (Cyclical)
- **Same-store sales growth (SSSG)**: For retail/restaurant chains. SSSG >5% = healthy. Negative SSSG = demand problem.
- **Consumer confidence**: Leading indicator for discretionary spending. Falling confidence = headwind for luxury, auto, electronics.
- **Wealth effect**: Rising equity markets and real estate prices boost discretionary spending. Market corrections reduce it.

## Real Estate & REITs

### Key Metrics
- **NAV (Net Asset Value)**: The value of underlying properties. Trading at discount to NAV = potentially undervalued. Premium to NAV = market pricing in growth.
- **FFO (Funds From Operations)**: The REIT equivalent of earnings. P/FFO is the primary valuation metric. P/FFO 15-20 = fair for diversified REITs.
- **Cap rate**: Net operating income / property value. Higher cap rate = higher yield but potentially higher risk. Cap rates compress when interest rates fall.
- **Occupancy rate**: >95% = strong demand. <90% = oversupply concern.

### Interest Rate Sensitivity
REITs are among the most rate-sensitive equity sectors. Rising rates = higher borrowing costs + competing yield from bonds. Falling rates = lower costs + REITs become relatively more attractive for yield.

## Interpreting Engine Scores by Sector

The same engine score means different things in different sectors:

| Score Pattern | Tech/Growth | Banks | Energy | Consumer Staples |
|:-------------|:-----------|:------|:-------|:----------------|
| T:80+ | Strong momentum, check if extended | Credit cycle expansion | Commodity price tailwind | Unusual — check for re-rating catalyst |
| V:70+ | Normal for high-beta tech | Concerning — banks should be stable | Normal during commodity volatility | Red flag — staples should be low-vol |
| L:40- | Concerning for any stock | Very concerning — banks need deep liquidity | Common for smaller E&P companies | Unusual — large-cap staples are liquid |
| M:30- | Growth deceleration — watch for trend break | Rate cycle turning against banks | Commodity price reversal | Defensive rotation OUT of staples (rare) |

### Sector-Regime Interaction
- **RISK_ON**: Tech, discretionary, small-caps outperform. Banks benefit from credit expansion.
- **DEFENSIVE**: Staples, utilities, healthcare outperform. High-beta tech underperforms.
- **RISK_OFF**: Cash, gold, short-duration bonds. Almost all equity sectors decline, but staples and healthcare decline less.
- **Sector dispersion**: High dispersion = sector selection matters. Low dispersion = macro dominates everything.
