## Key Concept

A useful watchlist is a **decision queue**, not a graveyard of interesting tickers. Its job is to help you revisit the right assets when context changes.

The best watchlists are small, intentional, and tied to a reason for monitoring.

## What It Means

- Add assets because you want to track a thesis, a setup, or a comparison.
- Revisit watchlist names when scores, narratives, or market events change.
- Use the watchlist to decide what deserves a fresh read — not to hoard symbols.
- If a name no longer matters to your process, remove it.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A big watchlist is **not** a better watchlist.
- Adding an asset does **not** mean it is ready to buy.
- Watchlist activity should **not** replace portfolio discipline or deeper review.

## Quick Check

- [x] A watchlist works best as a shortlist of assets you want to monitor with intent. | Correct — the point is focused monitoring, not collection.
- [ ] Adding an asset to a watchlist means it is automatically a strong opportunity. | No — it only means you want to keep tracking it.
- [x] Removing stale names can improve the quality of your watchlist. | Yes — clarity is part of the workflow.
- [ ] The best watchlist is the one with the most symbols in it. | No — signal quality usually improves when the list stays selective.
