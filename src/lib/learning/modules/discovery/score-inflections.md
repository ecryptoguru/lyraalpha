## Key Concept

A **Score Inflection** occurs when one or more of an asset's engine scores (Trend, Momentum, Volatility, Trust, Sentiment, Liquidity) shifts significantly over a short period — crossing a threshold that indicates a meaningful change in the asset's analytical profile. The Discovery feed surfaces these inflections because they represent *change*, which is more analytically valuable than static levels.

Think of it like a weather change: a temperature of 30°C is information, but a *drop* from 30°C to 15°C in two days is a signal. Score inflections capture these rate-of-change moments across all six analytical dimensions.

## What It Means

- **Positive inflection (score rising sharply)**: Conditions are improving in that dimension. A trend score jumping from 30 to 70 means directional consistency has strengthened dramatically. A trust score rising from 50 to 80 means governance or structural quality has improved.
- **Negative inflection (score falling sharply)**: Conditions are deteriorating. A liquidity score dropping from 85 to 45 means the market for this asset is drying up. A sentiment score collapsing from 70 to 20 means the crowd has turned bearish.
- **Multi-score inflection**: When multiple scores shift simultaneously, the signal is stronger. Three scores improving together suggests a broad-based improvement in the asset's profile, not just noise in one dimension.
- **Divergent inflection**: When some scores rise while others fall (e.g., momentum rising but trust falling), it creates analytical tension. These are the most interesting signals — they demand investigation.

The magnitude of the inflection matters. A 10-point shift is noise. A 30+ point shift in a week is a genuine signal that something has changed.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A score inflection is **not** a buy or sell signal. It's an alert that something changed — the direction and implications require further analysis.
- A positive inflection does **not** mean the asset is "good." A stock recovering from 10 to 40 on trend is still in weak territory — it's just less weak than before.
- Score inflections are **not** predictive. They describe what *has* changed, not what *will* happen next. The change might continue, reverse, or stabilize.
