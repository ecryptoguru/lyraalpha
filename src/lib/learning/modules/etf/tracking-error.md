## Key Concept

**Tracking Error** measures how much an ETF's actual returns deviate from its benchmark index. If an ETF tracks the S&P 500, a tracking error of 0.05% means the ETF's daily returns differ from the index by an average of 0.05%. Small numbers matter — over years, even tiny tracking errors compound.

Tracking error exists because ETFs have real-world costs that indexes don't: management fees, trading costs, cash drag (holding cash for redemptions), and sampling (not holding every stock in the index).

## What It Means

- **<0.10% annualized**: Excellent tracking. The ETF closely mirrors its benchmark. Typical of large, liquid ETFs from major issuers (e.g., SPY, QQQ, VTI).
- **0.10–0.50%**: Acceptable tracking. Minor deviations that most investors won't notice. Common in international and sector ETFs.
- **0.50–1.00%**: Noticeable tracking gap. The ETF is meaningfully underperforming its benchmark due to costs, sampling, or operational issues.
- **>1.00%**: Poor tracking. Something structural is wrong — high fees, illiquid underlying assets, or poor fund management. Investigate before investing.

Tracking error tends to be higher for ETFs that hold illiquid assets (emerging market bonds, small-cap international stocks) because the underlying securities are harder and more expensive to trade.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Low tracking error does **not** mean good returns. An ETF can perfectly track a declining index — you'll lose money with precision.
- Tracking error is **not** the same as expense ratio. Expense ratio is one *component* of tracking error, but trading costs, sampling, and securities lending income also contribute.
- A positive tracking difference (ETF beats its index) is **not** always good news. It might indicate the ETF is taking on additional risk through securities lending or derivatives.
