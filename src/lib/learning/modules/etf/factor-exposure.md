## Key Concept

**Factor Exposure** describes the systematic tilts embedded in an ETF's holdings. The five classic factors are **Value** (cheap stocks), **Growth** (fast-growing companies), **Momentum** (recent winners), **Quality** (profitable, low-debt firms), and **Size** (small vs large companies). Every ETF has factor exposures — even "plain" index funds.

Understanding factor exposure answers a critical question: *Why* is this ETF performing the way it is? If your "diversified" portfolio is secretly loaded with growth and momentum, you'll do great in bull markets and get crushed in rotations to value.

## What It Means

- **Value tilt**: The ETF overweights stocks trading at low price-to-earnings or price-to-book ratios. Value tends to outperform during economic recoveries and underperform during growth-led rallies.
- **Growth tilt**: Overweights companies with high revenue/earnings growth rates. Growth dominates in low-rate, tech-led environments but suffers when rates rise.
- **Momentum tilt**: Overweights recent winners. Momentum works until it doesn't — momentum crashes are sudden and severe.
- **Quality tilt**: Overweights profitable companies with strong balance sheets. Quality is defensive — it tends to hold up better during downturns.
- **Size tilt**: Small-cap overweight means higher return potential but also higher volatility and liquidity risk.

Many ETFs have *unintentional* factor exposures. A sector ETF focused on technology will naturally have a growth tilt. A dividend ETF will naturally tilt toward value and quality.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Factor exposure does **not** predict short-term returns. Factors work over long cycles (years), not weeks.
- Having a factor tilt is **not** inherently good or bad. It depends on whether the tilt aligns with your investment thesis and the current market regime.
- Factor labels are **not** permanent. An ETF's factor exposure can drift as its holdings change and market conditions evolve.

## Quick Check

- [x] Every ETF has factor exposures, even plain index funds. | Correct — market-cap weighted indexes naturally tilt toward large-cap and whatever factors dominate the largest stocks.
- [ ] A "quality" factor tilt guarantees the ETF will outperform in downturns. | No — quality tends to be more defensive, but no factor guarantees outperformance in any specific period.
- [x] An ETF's factor exposure can change over time as holdings and markets evolve. | Yes — factor exposures are dynamic. Rebalancing, market movements, and index methodology changes all affect factor tilts.
- [ ] Factor investing only matters for specialized "smart beta" ETFs. | Not true — even plain S&P 500 ETFs have significant factor exposures (currently heavy growth and momentum due to mega-cap tech dominance).
