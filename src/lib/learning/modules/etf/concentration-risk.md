## Key Concept

**Concentration Risk** measures how much of an ETF's value depends on its largest holdings. An ETF might hold 500 stocks but if the top 10 represent 35% of the fund, your "diversified" portfolio is really a bet on a handful of companies.

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI) quantifies this: it squares each holding's weight and sums them. A perfectly equal-weight 100-stock ETF has an HHI of 100. A top-heavy ETF where one stock is 20% might have an HHI of 600+. Higher HHI = more concentrated = more dependent on fewer names.

## What It Means

- **Top 10 weight >40%**: Highly concentrated. The ETF's performance is dominated by a small group. If those stocks stumble, the entire ETF suffers regardless of what the other 490 holdings do.
- **Top 10 weight 25–40%**: Moderately concentrated. Some dependence on top holdings, but the tail provides meaningful diversification.
- **Top 10 weight <25%**: Well-diversified. No single holding or small group dominates returns. True broad-market exposure.

Real example: The S&P 500 (SPY) has become increasingly concentrated — the "Magnificent 7" tech stocks now represent over 30% of the index. Buying SPY is partly a tech sector bet, whether you realize it or not.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High concentration does **not** automatically make an ETF bad. Concentrated ETFs can outperform when their top holdings are strong — it's a feature for investors who want that exposure intentionally.
- Low concentration does **not** guarantee safety. A well-diversified ETF in a declining sector will still lose money.
- Concentration risk is **not** just about the number of holdings. An ETF with 1,000 holdings can be more concentrated than one with 50 if the weights are skewed.
