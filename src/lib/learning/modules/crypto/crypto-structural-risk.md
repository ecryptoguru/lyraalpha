## Key Concept

**Structural risk** in crypto refers to vulnerabilities baked into a protocol's architecture — not its price action. Unlike stocks, where risk often comes from business performance, crypto structural risk comes from three dimensions:

1. **Dependency Risk** — How reliant is the protocol on a single blockchain, bridge, or oracle? A DeFi protocol that only runs on one chain has higher dependency risk than one deployed across multiple networks.
2. **Governance Risk** — How transparent and decentralized is the decision-making? Protocols where a small team controls upgrades, treasury, or token emissions carry higher governance risk.
3. **Maturity Risk** — How battle-tested is the protocol? Older protocols with years of operation and multiple market cycles have lower maturity risk than newly launched ones.

## What It Means

Each dimension is scored 0–100 and combined into an overall **Structural Risk** assessment:

- **Low structural risk**: Multi-chain deployment, transparent governance, years of operation, no major incidents. Think Bitcoin or Ethereum.
- **Moderate structural risk**: Some centralization in governance or single-chain dependency, but established track record. Many top-50 protocols fall here.
- **High structural risk**: Single-chain, opaque governance, recent launch, or history of exploits. Newer DeFi protocols and meme coins often score here.

The structural risk score is independent of price. A token can be pumping 200% while carrying critical structural risk — the two are not related.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Structural risk does **not** predict hacks or exploits. It identifies architectural patterns that historically correlate with higher incident rates.
- A low structural risk score does **not** mean the token is a good investment. Bitcoin has low structural risk but can still drop 50% in a bear market.
- This is **not** a security audit. Structural risk uses publicly available metadata (chain deployment, governance data, protocol age) — not smart contract code analysis.
- High structural risk does **not** mean "scam." Many legitimate early-stage protocols have high structural risk simply because they're new.

- [x] A newly launched protocol will typically have higher maturity risk than one that's been running for 5 years. | Yes — maturity risk reflects how battle-tested a protocol is across market cycles.
- [ ] If a token's price is rising, its structural risk must be low. | No — price and structural risk are independent. A token can pump while carrying critical architectural vulnerabilities.
