## Key Concept

The **Crypto Trust Model** combines six independent components into a single trust assessment for any crypto asset: **Protocol Security**, **Team Transparency**, **Decentralization**, **Regulatory Posture**, **Audit History**, and **Community Governance**. Each component is scored independently, then weighted to produce the overall trust score.

Unlike traditional equities where trust is anchored in audited financials and regulatory oversight, crypto trust must be constructed from on-chain evidence, protocol design, and community behavior. There is no SEC filing to rely on — you must verify trust through multiple independent signals.

## What It Means

- **Protocol Security**: Has the code been audited? Are there known vulnerabilities? Has the protocol survived attacks? Battle-tested protocols score higher.
- **Team Transparency**: Are the founders known? Is there a public development roadmap? Do they communicate regularly? Anonymous teams score lower (but aren't automatically untrustworthy).
- **Decentralization**: How distributed is validator/miner control? Can a small group halt or censor transactions? Higher decentralization = higher trust.
- **Regulatory Posture**: Has the project engaged with regulators? Are there legal opinions on token classification? Proactive compliance scores higher.
- **Audit History**: How many independent audits? Were findings addressed? Regular audits from reputable firms significantly boost trust.
- **Community Governance**: Do token holders have real voting power? Are governance proposals transparent? Active, decentralized governance increases trust.

The model is deliberately conservative — it's easier to lose trust points than to gain them, because in crypto, trust failures tend to be catastrophic and irreversible.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A high trust score does **not** mean the token will appreciate. Trust measures institutional quality, not market demand.
- A low trust score does **not** mean the project is a scam. New projects inherently have lower trust scores due to limited history — trust is earned over time.
- The trust model is **not** a replacement for your own research. It's a structured framework for evaluating trustworthiness, not a final verdict.
