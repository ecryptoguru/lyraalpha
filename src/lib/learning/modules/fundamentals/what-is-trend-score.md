## Key Concept

A **Trend Score** (0–100) measures how consistently an asset's price has been moving in one direction over recent weeks. It uses multiple moving averages, price position relative to key levels, and directional consistency — not just whether the price went up or down.

Think of it like a river's current: a high trend score means the water is flowing strongly in one direction. A low score means the current is weak or choppy.

## What It Means

- **80+**: Strong, sustained directional movement. The asset has been consistently trending (up or down) with conviction.
- **60–79**: Moderate trend. Direction is established but may be losing steam or just getting started.
- **40–59**: Neutral/mixed. No clear directional bias — the asset is range-bound or transitioning.
- **Below 40**: Weak or broken trend. The previous direction has stalled or reversed.

A high trend score combined with a declining momentum score is a classic "late trend" signal — the direction is still intact, but the rate of change is slowing.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A high trend score does **not** mean the price will continue going up. Trends can reverse at any time.
- A trend score does **not** tell you the direction — only the strength and consistency. A strong downtrend also produces a high trend score.
- It is **not** a buy or sell signal. It's one input among many that helps you understand the current state of price action.

## Quick Check

- [ ] A high trend score guarantees the price will keep rising. | No — trends measure consistency of direction, not future price movement. Strong downtrends also score high.
- [x] A trend score of 82 means the asset has been moving consistently in one direction. | Correct — it measures directional consistency across multiple timeframes.
- [ ] A trend score tells you whether to buy or sell. | No — it's an analytical input, not a trading signal. Always consider other scores and context.
- [x] A falling trend score might indicate the current direction is weakening. | Yes — declining trend scores often signal that the prevailing move is losing conviction.
