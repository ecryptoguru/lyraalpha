## Key Concept

A **Liquidity Score** (0–100) measures how easily an asset can be bought or sold without significantly moving its price. It combines trading volume, bid-ask spread analysis, and volume consistency over time.

Think of it like a busy marketplace vs a quiet alley shop. In the marketplace (high liquidity), you can buy or sell quickly at fair prices. In the alley shop (low liquidity), you might wait a long time or accept a worse price.

## What It Means

- **80+**: Highly liquid. Large positions can be entered or exited with minimal price impact. Typical of mega-cap stocks, major ETFs, and top-10 crypto assets.
- **60–79**: Good liquidity. Normal-sized trades execute smoothly, but very large orders may move the price slightly.
- **40–59**: Moderate liquidity. Adequate for most retail investors, but institutional-sized trades would face slippage.
- **Below 40**: Low liquidity. Wider bid-ask spreads, potential difficulty exiting positions quickly. Common in small-cap stocks, niche ETFs, and lower-ranked crypto.

Liquidity can evaporate during market stress — assets that are normally liquid can become illiquid precisely when you need to sell most.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High liquidity does **not** mean the asset is a good investment. The most liquid assets in the world can still lose value.
- Low liquidity does **not** mean the asset is bad. Many excellent small-cap companies have lower liquidity — it just means position sizing and entry/exit timing matter more.
- Liquidity is **not** static. It changes with market conditions, time of day, and macro events. A liquidity score is a snapshot, not a guarantee.

## Quick Check

- [x] Liquidity measures how easily you can buy or sell without moving the price. | Correct — it captures the depth and efficiency of the market for that asset.
- [ ] A highly liquid asset is always a safe investment. | No — liquidity means you can trade easily, not that the price won't decline. They are independent concepts.
- [x] Liquidity can decrease during market stress events. | Yes — this is a critical risk. Assets that seem liquid in calm markets can become very hard to sell during panics.
- [ ] Low liquidity scores only apply to obscure assets. | Not true — even well-known assets can have low liquidity during off-hours, in certain markets, or during extreme volatility.
