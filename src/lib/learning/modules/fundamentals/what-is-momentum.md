## Key Concept

A **Momentum Score** (0–100) measures the *rate of change* in an asset's price — how fast it's moving, not just which direction. While trend measures consistency of direction, momentum measures acceleration and deceleration.

Think of a car on a highway: trend tells you the car is heading north, momentum tells you whether it's speeding up or slowing down.

## What It Means

- **80+**: Strong acceleration. Price is moving rapidly in its current direction. This often occurs during breakouts, earnings surprises, or macro catalysts.
- **60–79**: Healthy momentum. The asset is gaining ground at a sustainable pace.
- **40–59**: Neutral. Neither accelerating nor decelerating meaningfully. Often seen in consolidation phases.
- **Below 40**: Weak or fading momentum. The current move is losing energy. This doesn't mean reversal is imminent — it means the fuel behind the move is depleting.

A key signal: when momentum diverges from trend (e.g., high trend but falling momentum), it often indicates the current move is in its later stages.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High momentum does **not** mean the asset is a good buy. Momentum can be strong in a downtrend too — the asset is falling fast.
- Falling momentum does **not** guarantee a reversal. Assets can drift sideways with low momentum for extended periods.
- Momentum is **not** the same as volatility. An asset can have high momentum (moving fast in one direction) with low volatility (doing so smoothly).

## Quick Check

- [x] Momentum measures how fast price is changing, not just the direction. | Correct — momentum captures the rate of change, which is distinct from trend direction.
- [ ] High momentum always means the asset is going up. | No — momentum measures speed of movement in any direction. A crashing stock has high downward momentum.
- [x] Divergence between trend and momentum can signal a late-stage move. | Yes — when trend is high but momentum is fading, the current move may be running out of steam.
- [ ] Low momentum means the asset is about to reverse. | Not necessarily — low momentum can persist during extended consolidation periods without any reversal.
