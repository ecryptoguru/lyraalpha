## Key Concept

A **Sentiment Score** (0–100) measures the overall market mood surrounding an asset — derived from news flow, analyst commentary, social media activity, and volume-price patterns. It captures what the crowd *thinks* about an asset, not what the asset is actually doing.

Think of it as a thermometer for market opinion. High sentiment means the crowd is enthusiastic; low sentiment means fear or disinterest. Neither is inherently good or bad.

## What It Means

- **80+**: Extremely bullish sentiment. The crowd is overwhelmingly positive. Historically, extreme bullish sentiment can precede pullbacks — "when everyone is bullish, who's left to buy?"
- **60–79**: Positive sentiment. Generally favorable market opinion, but not at extreme levels.
- **40–59**: Neutral sentiment. Mixed opinions, no strong consensus. Often the most analytically useful zone — the crowd hasn't made up its mind.
- **Below 40**: Bearish or fearful sentiment. The crowd is negative. Contrarian investors often find opportunities here, but sentiment can stay low for extended periods.

The most powerful signals often come from *changes* in sentiment — a sudden shift from 30 to 60 is more meaningful than a steady 70.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High sentiment does **not** mean the asset will go up. Markets frequently peak when sentiment is most bullish.
- Low sentiment does **not** mean the asset will go down further. Some of the best buying opportunities occur during peak pessimism.
- Sentiment is **not** fundamental analysis. It measures what people *feel*, not what the numbers *show*. A company with terrible fundamentals can have high sentiment (hype), and vice versa.
