## Key Concept

A **Volatility Score** (0–100) measures how much an asset's price fluctuates relative to its recent history and its peers. High volatility means larger price swings — both up and down. Low volatility means the price moves in a tighter, more predictable range.

Volatility is often misunderstood as "risk." It's more accurate to think of it as **uncertainty** — the range of possible outcomes is wider when volatility is high.

## What It Means

- **80+**: Extremely volatile. The asset is experiencing large daily swings. This could mean opportunity for some, but it also means the price could move sharply against you without warning.
- **60–79**: Elevated volatility. Larger-than-normal price movements. Often seen during earnings seasons, macro events, or sector rotations.
- **40–59**: Normal range. Price movements are within historical norms for this asset type.
- **Below 40**: Low volatility. The asset is unusually calm. This can precede a breakout (in either direction) or simply reflect a stable, mature asset.

Crypto assets naturally have higher baseline volatility than blue-chip stocks. A volatility score of 60 for Bitcoin might be "calm" by crypto standards but would be alarming for a utility stock.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High volatility does **not** mean the asset is "risky" in the fundamental sense. A volatile stock with strong earnings and a solid balance sheet is different from a volatile meme coin.
- Low volatility does **not** mean "safe." Some of the largest market crashes were preceded by periods of unusually low volatility (the "calm before the storm" pattern).
- Volatility is **not** directional. A volatility score of 85 doesn't tell you whether the asset will go up or down — only that the magnitude of moves is large.
