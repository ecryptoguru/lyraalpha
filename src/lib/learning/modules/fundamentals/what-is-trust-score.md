## Key Concept

A **Trust Score** (0–100) assesses the institutional quality, governance, and reliability of an asset. For stocks, it evaluates financial health, audit quality, and corporate governance. For crypto, it examines protocol security, team transparency, and decentralization. For ETFs and mutual funds, it looks at issuer reputation, regulatory compliance, and operational track record.

Think of it as a background check on the asset itself — not whether it will go up, but whether the entity behind it is trustworthy and well-managed.

## What It Means

- **80+**: High trust. Strong governance, transparent reporting, established track record. Blue-chip stocks, major ETF issuers, and battle-tested crypto protocols typically score here.
- **60–79**: Good trust. Generally well-managed with minor concerns. Most mid-cap stocks and established crypto projects fall in this range.
- **40–59**: Moderate trust. Some governance concerns, limited track record, or structural issues worth monitoring. Due diligence is especially important here.
- **Below 40**: Low trust. Significant governance red flags, opaque reporting, or structural vulnerabilities. This doesn't mean fraud — it means the risk of unpleasant surprises is elevated.

Trust scores change slowly compared to other scores. A sudden drop in trust is a serious signal that warrants immediate attention.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A high trust score does **not** mean the asset will perform well. Well-governed companies can still have declining businesses.
- A low trust score does **not** mean the asset is a scam. Early-stage companies and newer crypto protocols often have lower trust scores simply due to limited history.
- Trust is **not** a substitute for fundamental analysis. It's one dimension of risk — the "can I rely on this entity?" dimension — not a comprehensive assessment.

## Quick Check

- [x] Trust scores assess governance and institutional quality, not price performance. | Correct — trust measures reliability and transparency, which is independent of whether the price goes up or down.
- [ ] A trust score of 90 means the asset is a safe investment. | No — high trust means the entity is well-governed, not that the investment is risk-free. Even trusted companies can lose value.
- [ ] Trust scores change as frequently as momentum or trend scores. | No — trust scores are slow-moving. They reflect structural qualities that change over quarters, not days.
- [x] A sudden drop in trust score is a serious warning signal. | Yes — because trust changes slowly, a sudden decline suggests something meaningful has changed in governance, transparency, or structural integrity.
