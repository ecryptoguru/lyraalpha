## Key Concept

The **Expense Ratio** is the annual fee a mutual fund charges as a percentage of your invested assets. A 1.5% expense ratio means for every ₹1,00,000 invested, ₹1,500 is deducted annually — regardless of whether the fund makes or loses money. Over time, this seemingly small number compounds dramatically.

The critical insight: expense ratios don't just reduce your returns — they reduce the *base* on which future returns compound. A 1% higher expense ratio over 20 years can reduce your final portfolio value by 18-20%.

## What It Means

- **<0.50%**: Low cost. Typical of index funds and large passive ETFs. Your returns closely track the underlying assets.
- **0.50–1.00%**: Moderate cost. Common for actively managed funds. The fund manager needs to outperform the benchmark by at least this much just to break even with a cheaper alternative.
- **1.00–2.00%**: High cost. The fund must significantly outperform to justify the fee. Most actively managed equity funds in India fall here.
- **>2.00%**: Very high cost. Extremely difficult to justify over long periods. Only a tiny fraction of funds consistently outperform their benchmark by this margin.

Always compare **Direct Plan** vs **Regular Plan** expense ratios. The difference (typically 0.5–1.0%) is the commission paid to distributors. Direct plans are always cheaper for the same fund.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- A low expense ratio does **not** guarantee better returns. A skilled active manager charging 1.5% might outperform a 0.1% index fund — but statistically, most don't over long periods.
- A high expense ratio does **not** mean the fund is bad. Some niche strategies (international, small-cap, sectoral) have legitimately higher operating costs.
- Expense ratio is **not** the only cost. Exit loads, transaction costs, and tax inefficiency also affect your net returns.
