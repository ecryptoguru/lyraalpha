## Key Concept

**Portfolio Overlap** measures how much two or more mutual funds hold the same underlying stocks. If you own three large-cap funds that each hold HDFC Bank, Reliance, and Infosys as top positions, you don't have three diversified bets — you have one concentrated bet spread across three fee structures.

Overlap is measured as a percentage: 70% overlap between Fund A and Fund B means 70% of your money in those two funds is invested in the same stocks. The higher the overlap, the less diversification benefit you get from holding both.

## What It Means

- **Overlap >70%**: Near-identical portfolios. Holding both funds provides almost no diversification benefit. You're paying two expense ratios for essentially the same exposure.
- **Overlap 40–70%**: Significant overlap. Some diversification benefit exists, but less than you might expect. Worth evaluating whether both funds are necessary.
- **Overlap 20–40%**: Moderate overlap. Reasonable diversification between the two funds. Some common holdings are expected within the same market.
- **Overlap <20%**: Genuinely different portfolios. Holding both provides real diversification across different stocks, sectors, or styles.

The most common overlap trap: holding multiple large-cap or flexi-cap funds from different AMCs. Because the Indian large-cap universe is concentrated (top 20 stocks dominate), most large-cap funds end up holding similar portfolios.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- High overlap does **not** mean either fund is bad. Both might be excellent — you just don't need both.
- Low overlap does **not** guarantee better returns. Two non-overlapping funds can both underperform.
- Overlap analysis is **not** a one-time exercise. Fund portfolios change quarterly, so overlap percentages shift over time.
