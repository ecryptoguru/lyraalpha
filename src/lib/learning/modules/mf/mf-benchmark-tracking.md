## Key Concept

**Benchmark Tracking** compares a mutual fund's actual returns against its stated benchmark index. Every active fund claims to beat its benchmark — but how do you verify? By measuring **Active Share** (how different the portfolio is from the index) and **Information Ratio** (risk-adjusted excess return).

The dirty secret of the mutual fund industry: many "active" funds are **closet indexers** — they charge active management fees (1-2%) while holding portfolios that look almost identical to the benchmark. You're paying for active management but getting passive returns minus higher fees.

## What It Means

- **Active Share >80%**: Truly active. The fund's holdings differ significantly from the benchmark. The manager is making real bets — for better or worse.
- **Active Share 60–80%**: Moderately active. Some differentiation from the benchmark, but significant overlap remains.
- **Active Share <60%**: Closet indexer. The fund closely mirrors its benchmark despite charging active fees. Consider switching to a cheaper index fund.
- **Information Ratio >0.5**: The fund is generating meaningful risk-adjusted excess returns. The active management is adding value.
- **Information Ratio <0**: The fund is underperforming its benchmark on a risk-adjusted basis. The active management is destroying value.

Check rolling 3-year and 5-year periods, not just the latest year. One good year doesn't prove skill — consistency does.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Beating the benchmark in one year does **not** prove the manager is skilled. Short-term outperformance is often luck, not skill.
- High Active Share does **not** guarantee outperformance. A manager can be very different from the benchmark and still lose money.
- Underperforming the benchmark does **not** always mean the fund is bad. In certain market conditions, defensive positioning may lag but protect capital.

## Quick Check

- [x] Closet indexers charge active management fees while closely mirroring their benchmark. | Correct — they have low Active Share but high expense ratios, giving you the worst of both worlds.
- [ ] A fund that beat its benchmark last year is definitely worth the higher fees. | No — one year of outperformance doesn't prove skill. Look at rolling 3-5 year periods for consistency.
- [x] Active Share measures how different a fund's holdings are from its benchmark. | Yes — higher Active Share means the manager is making more independent decisions, deviating further from the index.
- [ ] All actively managed funds are closet indexers. | Not true — many funds have genuinely high Active Share and make real active bets. The key is identifying which ones.
