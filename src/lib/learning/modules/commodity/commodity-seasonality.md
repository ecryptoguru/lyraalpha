## Key Concept

**Commodity Seasonality** refers to recurring patterns in commodity prices that align with calendar periods — driven by weather cycles, planting/harvest seasons, industrial demand cycles, and energy consumption patterns. Unlike stock market "seasonality" (which is mostly statistical noise), commodity seasonality has real physical drivers.

Natural gas prices tend to rise before winter (heating demand). Agricultural commodities move with planting and harvest cycles. Gasoline prices often peak before summer driving season. These patterns are well-documented but not guaranteed — they represent historical tendencies, not certainties.

## What It Means

- **Energy**: Natural gas typically strengthens October–January (winter heating) and weakens March–May. Crude oil often sees strength before summer driving season (April–June).
- **Agriculture**: Grain prices tend to be volatile during planting (March–May) and harvest (September–November) in the Northern Hemisphere. Weather uncertainty during growing season creates price spikes.
- **Precious Metals**: Gold often sees demand increases during Indian wedding season (October–December) and Chinese New Year. These cultural demand patterns create measurable price tendencies.
- **Industrial Metals**: Copper and aluminum often strengthen during construction season (spring/summer) in major economies.

The key insight: seasonality works best as a *context layer*, not a trading strategy. If seasonal patterns align with other signals (trend, momentum, regime), the combined signal is stronger.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Seasonal patterns are **not** predictions. They represent historical averages — any individual year can deviate significantly due to weather anomalies, geopolitical events, or demand shifts.
- Seasonality does **not** override fundamental supply/demand dynamics. A warm winter will suppress natural gas prices regardless of the "typical" seasonal pattern.
- Past seasonal performance is **not** a guarantee of future results. Climate change, shifting trade patterns, and energy transitions are altering historical seasonal norms.
