## Key Concept

**Contango** and **Backwardation** describe the shape of a commodity's futures curve — the relationship between prices for delivery today (spot) versus delivery in the future. This structure has a direct, measurable impact on the returns of commodity ETFs and futures-based investments.

- **Contango**: Future prices are *higher* than spot prices. The futures curve slopes upward. This is the "normal" state for most commodities because storage, insurance, and financing costs are built into future prices.
- **Backwardation**: Future prices are *lower* than spot prices. The futures curve slopes downward. This typically occurs when there's immediate scarcity — the market is willing to pay a premium for delivery *now*.

## What It Means

- **Contango creates a "roll cost"**: Commodity ETFs that hold futures must periodically sell expiring contracts and buy the next month's contract. In contango, the next month is more expensive — so you're selling low and buying high every month. This silent drag can cost 5-15% annually.
- **Backwardation creates "roll yield"**: The opposite — you sell the expiring contract at a higher price than you pay for the next one. This positive roll yield adds to returns beyond the commodity's spot price movement.
- **Steep contango (>5% annualized)**: A major headwind for futures-based commodity investments. The commodity's spot price needs to rise by more than the roll cost just to break even.
- **Deep backwardation**: Often signals supply stress. The market desperately wants the commodity *now*. This can precede price spikes but also indicates potential supply disruptions.

This is why commodity ETF returns often diverge significantly from the commodity's spot price over time. The futures curve structure matters as much as the price direction.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Contango does **not** mean the commodity price will fall. It simply means future delivery costs more than spot — a reflection of carrying costs, not a directional prediction.
- Backwardation does **not** mean the commodity is a guaranteed buy. While it creates favorable roll yield, the spot price can still decline.
- Futures curve structure is **not** relevant for physical commodity holders. Contango and backwardation only affect investors who access commodities through futures or futures-based ETFs.
