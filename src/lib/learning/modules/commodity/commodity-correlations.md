## Key Concept

**Commodity Correlations** measure how commodity prices move relative to each other and to broader financial markets. Some commodities move together (crude oil and natural gas), some move inversely (gold and the US dollar), and some have shifting relationships that depend on the macro environment.

Understanding these relationships is essential because commodities don't exist in isolation. When oil rises, energy stocks follow. When gold rises, it often signals risk aversion. When copper rises, it's sometimes called "Dr. Copper" because it diagnoses economic health — industrial demand drives copper prices.

## What It Means

- **Positive correlation (oil ↔ energy stocks)**: These assets tend to move in the same direction. Owning both provides less diversification than you might think.
- **Negative correlation (gold ↔ USD)**: These assets tend to move in opposite directions. Gold is often a hedge against dollar weakness.
- **Shifting correlation (oil ↔ equities)**: Sometimes correlated, sometimes not. During supply shocks, oil rises while stocks fall. During demand-driven rallies, both rise together. The *reason* behind the move determines the correlation.
- **Breakdown correlation**: During extreme market stress, correlations tend to converge — everything sells off together as investors liquidate for cash. The diversification benefit of commodities can temporarily disappear precisely when you need it most.

The most important insight: correlations are not fixed. They change with market regimes, monetary policy, and geopolitical conditions. A correlation calculated over the last 5 years may not hold in the next 6 months.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Historical correlation does **not** guarantee future correlation. Regime changes can flip relationships entirely.
- Negative correlation does **not** mean perfect hedging. Gold doesn't always rise when stocks fall — it's a tendency, not a rule.
- Commodity correlations are **not** simple or static. The same two commodities can be positively correlated in one regime and negatively correlated in another.

- [x] Commodity correlations shift depending on market regime and macro conditions. | Correct — relationships between commodities and other assets are dynamic, not fixed.
- [ ] Gold always rises when the stock market falls. | No — gold tends to rise during risk-off periods, but it's a tendency, not a guarantee. In liquidity crises, even gold can sell off.

- [ ] If two commodities were correlated last year, they will be correlated next year. | Not necessarily — regime changes, policy shifts, and supply disruptions can alter or reverse historical correlations.
