## Key Concept

**Cross-Asset Correlation** measures how different asset classes — equities, bonds, commodities, crypto, currencies — move relative to each other. These relationships are not fixed; they shift with market regimes, monetary policy, and macro conditions. Understanding *when* and *why* correlations change is one of the most valuable analytical skills in multi-asset investing.

The traditional assumption — "stocks and bonds are negatively correlated" — held for decades but broke down in 2022 when both fell simultaneously. This is not an anomaly; it's a regime-dependent relationship. In inflationary environments, stocks and bonds can become positively correlated, destroying the diversification benefit that most portfolios rely on.

## What It Means

- **Stable correlations**: When cross-asset relationships hold steady, portfolio construction is straightforward. Diversification works as expected. This is the "normal" state during established regimes.
- **Correlation breakdown**: When historically stable relationships change, it signals a regime shift. The 60/40 portfolio's failure in 2022 was a correlation breakdown — bonds stopped hedging equity risk.
- **Correlation convergence**: During crises, correlations spike toward +1 as investors sell everything for cash. This is the most dangerous state — diversification fails precisely when you need it most.
- **Leading correlations**: Some cross-asset relationships lead others. Credit spreads widening before equity declines, copper weakening before industrial stocks, or the yield curve inverting before recessions — these are leading signals embedded in cross-asset behavior.

The analytical framework: track rolling 30-day and 90-day correlations between key pairs (stocks/bonds, gold/USD, crypto/equities, oil/inflation expectations). When short-term correlations diverge sharply from long-term norms, something is changing.

## What It Means for Your Portfolio

- **Positive stock-bond correlation**: Your "balanced" portfolio has less diversification than you think. Consider adding uncorrelated alternatives (commodities, trend-following strategies).
- **Rising equity-crypto correlation**: Crypto is behaving as a risk asset, not a diversifier. Don't count on it for portfolio protection.
- **Gold decorrelating from USD**: Gold may be responding to geopolitical or monetary factors beyond simple dollar dynamics. Investigate the driver.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Historical correlations are **not** guarantees. The relationship between any two asset classes can change, sometimes permanently.
- Low correlation does **not** mean zero risk. Two uncorrelated assets can both lose money simultaneously — they just don't tend to move in lockstep.
- Cross-asset analysis is **not** a timing tool. It tells you about the *structure* of market relationships, not when specific assets will move.
