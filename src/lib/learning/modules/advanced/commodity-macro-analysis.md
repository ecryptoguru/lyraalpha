## Key Concept

**Commodity Macro Analysis** examines commodities through the lens of global macro dynamics — monetary policy, USD strength, real interest rates, global growth expectations, and geopolitical supply risks. At the advanced level, commodity prices are not just about supply and demand for the physical good; they're expressions of macro themes playing out across the global economy.

The three macro pillars that drive commodity prices:
1. **USD dynamics**: Most commodities are priced in dollars. A weaker dollar makes commodities cheaper for non-US buyers, boosting demand. A stronger dollar does the opposite. The DXY (Dollar Index) is one of the most important inputs for commodity analysis.
2. **Real interest rates**: When real rates (nominal rates minus inflation) are negative, holding commodities has a lower opportunity cost — you're not giving up meaningful yield by owning gold instead of bonds. Negative real rates historically coincide with commodity bull markets.
3. **Global growth expectations**: Industrial commodities (copper, oil, iron ore) are proxies for global economic activity. When PMIs are expanding globally, industrial commodity demand rises. When growth slows, demand contracts.

## What It Means

- **Weak USD + negative real rates + expanding PMIs**: The most bullish macro configuration for commodities. All three pillars support higher prices. This environment favors broad commodity exposure.
- **Strong USD + positive real rates + contracting PMIs**: The most bearish configuration. Commodity headwinds across all dimensions. Only supply-shock-driven commodities (geopolitical disruptions) tend to perform.
- **Mixed signals**: When pillars conflict (e.g., weak USD but contracting PMIs), commodity performance becomes selective. Precious metals may benefit from the weak dollar while industrial metals suffer from weak growth.
- **Geopolitical supply premium**: Wars, sanctions, and trade disruptions can override macro fundamentals. The Russia-Ukraine conflict's impact on energy and grain prices demonstrated how supply shocks can dominate all other factors.

The advanced insight: commodity cycles tend to be longer than equity cycles. A commodity supercycle (sustained multi-year bull market) is driven by structural underinvestment in supply meeting rising demand — and these imbalances take years to resolve.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Macro analysis does **not** predict short-term commodity prices. Daily moves are driven by positioning, technical levels, and news flow. Macro sets the direction over quarters and years.
- A bullish macro setup does **not** mean all commodities will rise equally. Each commodity has its own supply/demand dynamics that can override macro tailwinds.
- Commodity macro analysis is **not** a substitute for understanding the physical market. Inventory levels, production capacity, and weather still matter enormously.
