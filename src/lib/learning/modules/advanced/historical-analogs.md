## Key Concept

**Historical Analogs** are past market episodes that share structural similarities with current conditions. By identifying periods with similar regime characteristics, cross-asset correlations, volatility patterns, and macro backdrops, you can build a probabilistic framework for what might happen next — not as a prediction, but as a range of scenarios informed by precedent.

The key principle: history doesn't repeat, but it rhymes. The 2022 inflation shock rhymed with 1970s stagflation. The 2020 COVID crash rhymed with 1987's sudden liquidity crisis. The 2024 AI rally rhymed with 1999's tech euphoria. Each analog is imperfect, but the structural similarities provide analytical value.

## What It Means

- **Identifying analogs**: Match current conditions across multiple dimensions — regime type, volatility level, yield curve shape, credit spread level, sector leadership, and cross-asset correlation structure. The more dimensions that match, the stronger the analog.
- **Using analogs**: Study what happened *after* the analog period. What triggered the transition? How long did the regime persist? Which asset classes led the recovery or decline? What were the false signals?
- **Multiple analogs**: Never rely on a single analog. Identify 3-5 historical periods that share similarities, then look for *common patterns* across all of them. If 4 out of 5 analogs saw credit spreads widen before equities fell, that's a robust signal.
- **Analog divergence**: When current conditions start deviating from the analog, it's equally informative. The *differences* between now and then reveal what's unique about the current environment.

The most valuable analogs are not the obvious ones. Everyone compares the current market to 2008 or 2000. The less-discussed analogs — 1994's bond market massacre, 2015's manufacturing recession, 2018's quantitative tightening — often provide more actionable insights because they're less crowded.

## What It Does NOT Mean

- Historical analogs are **not** predictions. They provide probabilistic scenarios, not certainties. The future always contains elements that have no historical precedent.
- A strong analog match does **not** mean the outcome will be identical. Different policy responses, market structures, and global conditions can produce different results from similar starting points.
- Analogs are **not** a substitute for real-time analysis. They provide context and scenarios, but current data always takes priority over historical patterns.
